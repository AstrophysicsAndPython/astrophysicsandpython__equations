What is Schwarzschild radius?

Schwarzschild radius is such a radius that if an object is compressed down to it, the object becomes so dense that even light cannot escape from within. The Schwarzschild radius (\(R_s\)) is a coordinate singularity in the solution to Einstein's field equations which separates the interior and exterior space-time of the compressed object (black hole). For the particular coordinates used by Schwarzschild in obtaining the solution, \(r = R_s\) does produce the singularity. However, this singularity can be removed if we shift to a different coordinate system.

Formula

Although we will not be going in details about how to derive the solution proposed by Karl Schwarzschild, we will outline the formula where the singularity appears and explain it a bit for understanding purposes.
Assuming a spherically symmetric and static spacetime without the presence of a cosmological constant, the space-time metric can be simplified down to,

\(ds^2 = A(r)dr^2 + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta d\phi^2) + B(r)dt^2 \)

Note that, \(A(r)\) and \(B(r)\) are two functions that are entirely \(r\) dependant. So if, at any point, the radial element becomes 0, the metric becomes singular. Further, simplifying the expressions for \(A(r)\) and \(B(r)\) gives us,

\(ds^2 = \left(1 - \dfrac{2GM}{c^2r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta d\phi^2) - c^2\left(1 - \dfrac{2GM}{c^2r}\right)dt^2 \)

The expression in the parenthesis \(\dfrac{2GM}{c^2}\) is the expression for Schwarzschild radius. Rewriting the above equation,

\(ds^2 = \left(1 - \dfrac{R_s}{r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta d\phi^2) - c^2\left(1 - \dfrac{R_s}{r}\right)dt^2 \)

The terms, \(\left(1 - \dfrac{R_s}{r}\right)^{-1}\) and \(\left(1 - \dfrac{R_s}{r}\right)\) becomes 0 at \(r = R_s\) and \(r = 0\) respectively thus making the above equation singular at two different points.

Now that we know about that the Schwarzschild radius can be obtained by,

\(R_s = \dfrac{2GM}{c^2}\)
